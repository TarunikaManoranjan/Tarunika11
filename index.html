Evolution of Pacemakers (How They Changed Over Time)

Pacemakers have improved a lot since they were first created. Here’s how they developed over time:

Early Pacemakers (1930s–1950s)

  • The first pacemakers were big and used outside the body.
  • In 1958, doctors in Sweden made the first implantable pacemaker. It didn’t last long, but it was the beginning of modern pacemakers.

Better Designs (1960s–1980s)

  • New pacemakers had longer battery life (thanks to lithium batteries).
  • Some could pace two heart chambers instead of just one.
  • They became smaller and more reliable.

Modern Pacemakers (1990s–Today)

  • Now pacemakers are very small and can be monitored remotely.
  • Some are even MRI-safe.
  • New types like leadless pacemakers don’t need wires at all.

Two Types of Pacemakers

1. Single-Chamber Pacemaker

What it does: Sends signals to just one part of the heart—either the upper part (atrium) or the lower part (ventricle).

When it’s used: For people whose heart beats too slowly, and only one chamber needs help.

Why it's good:

  • Simple to put in
  • Fewer problems because it has only one wire

Limitations:

  • Doesn’t work as well for people who need both chambers paced

2. Leadless Pacemaker

What it is: A very small pacemaker that goes directly into the heart—no wires or surgery needed on the chest.

When it’s used: For people who might have problems with wires or infections.

Why it's good:

  • No scars
  • Lower chance of infection
  • Fast recovery

Limitations:

  • Works only for the lower part of the heart (ventricle)
  • Can be hard to remove if there’s a problem
